An Introduction to Me

Standing in a newly-established coffee shop, I was intrigued by its unique layout; I was stepping up ramps rather than steps, and using a button to unlock the entrance. The shop felt vacant, with lots of open space, and the menu was written in bold green lettering. "They should definitely add additional tables and make the menu more appealing," I thought to myself. But when I saw a man in a wheelchair exiting the all-access restroom, it all made sense; each feature was designed to assist disabled customers and employees. Looking back, I saw the experience of entering differently. I couldn't fathom how difficult it would be for a wheelchair user to go shopping without ramps, have to force open doors, and have no space to move. I was glad that the owner and a deaf barista, Dennis, were willing to share their stories when I asked. Dennis described how difficult it was for him to maintain his life after losing most of his hearing in an accident. Before this barista position, he’d struggled to find a job or pay his utility bills for months. The owner pitched in and explained that he founded this shop because he had seen similar things happen to many disabled people and wanted to help them land on their feet. I was especially drawn to the owner’s idea of building a Barrier-Free society and decided I too could help people who were still struggling to find a job or even afraid to leave their homes to have better lives, as Dennis does. I asked Dennis to accompany me to the Disabled Persons' Federation's annual conference meeting so I could learn more. I chatted with ten people after the meeting, and the most memorable one was a guy with severe spinal injuries who was part of the group that crafted cashmere flowers given to Winter Olympics participants as gifts. He struggled to find another job for four months, and the jobs he eventually got were menial and didn't last long even though he was eager and capable of doing more. As I listened, I was saddened by the obstacles they faced and heartened by their hopeful approach. To gather more information, I constructed and distributed a questionnaire with 30 questions, including their basic information, disability types, living conditions, and anything they needed help with. From what I gathered, the primary goals they sought included on-time government subsidies, better-managed support facilities, job opportunities, and, most notably, being treated with respect. To raise awareness and determine how to use my abilities to help the disabled community, I put together a presentation for our school's community service sessions based on the input from the questionnaires. Among many other plans, I wanted to create an app devoted to assisting disabled people with finding jobs. I formed a team of three students to carry out the outlined processes, including coding, obtaining Federation approval, and locating and persuading firms to post job opportunities. The hardest part was convincing employers. After repeatedly being turned away, we were finally able to convince a small number of employers with a presentation that included a video of disabled workers doing their jobs to show how good they were, a list of government incentives that would give cash or tax deductions to companies that hired disabled workers, and the results of interviews and surveys about how hiring disabled workers would improve the companies' reputations. I won't forget Dennis' smile when I told him about my modest win of two hires and three interviews one month after the app's launch, and I wanted to make that smile last by continually improving the app. We have plans to implement voice control and other UI improvements tailored to different disabilities while gathering the necessary knowledge. With the app's growing traffic and pending promotion plans from the Disabled Persons' Federation, the platform could help many more people.
